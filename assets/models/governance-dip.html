The Governance Dip — Why MRM Gets Worse Before It Gets Much Better

Every governance build-out feels like punishment for trying to do the right thing. The pain is real — but it is a valley, not a cliff. On the other side, the same controls that hurt are what make velocity compound.

The shape. Anyone who has been through a serious governance build-out knows the shape. Things were fine, more or less, before. Then the program lands — the committees, the validators, the sign-off chains, the documentation templates — and for a long time it gets worse. Velocity drops. Engineers grumble. Auditors are still unhappy. The sponsors who funded the program start asking when, exactly, the value shows up.

Why it has to dip. The first half of the curve is everything you have to put in place before the system starts paying you back. Independent validators do not generate models; they slow models down. A separation-of-duties matrix is a calendar of handoffs. A sign-off chain is a queue. A version-control mandate is a meeting about a meeting. None of those things are productive on their first day. They are scaffolding — and scaffolding has a cost before the building does.

The bottom is the trap. Most programs stall at the bottom. The controls are in place, the pain is real, the value has not landed, and the easiest political move is to declare "MRM done" and stop investing. This is the check-the-box equilibrium: every control exists, every checklist gets initialed, and nothing actually compounds. The diagram on this page is mostly a warning about that bottom.

What turns the curve. Past the trough, the same controls that hurt start paying back — but only when they stop being one-off paperwork and start being primitives. Lineage that's a chore on day one is automatic by month twelve. A validation pattern that took a quarter to write is a button by year two. Self-service governed environments mean a new modeler is producing on day three, not day ninety. The work was the same; the leverage is finally there.

The right side of the curve. Past zero, the reward is not just back to where we were. It is materially better than before MRM, because every model now ships with auditable lineage built in, every team can re-use a validated pattern, and the cost of compliance is amortised across the platform instead of paid by each project. That is the shape of the Domino value proposition: the dip is unavoidable, but the slope after it is steep, and the height it reaches is permanent.

The X-axis. Governance build-out is shorthand for: standing up the org, hiring validators, writing the controls map, codifying the sign-off chain, and — most of all — putting a platform under the controls so they execute themselves. The right end of the axis is not "more meetings". It is a system where the right thing is the easy thing.
